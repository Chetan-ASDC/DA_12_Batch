# Lecture – Part 1: Foundations of Inferential Statistics

---

## 1. What is Inferential Statistics?

### Meaning 
Inferential statistics is the branch of statistics that helps us **draw conclusions about a large group** (called population) by studying a **small part of it** (called sample).

In simple terms, we **use sample data to predict or make decisions about the population**.

---

### Why Inferential Statistics is Needed
Studying the entire population is often:
- Time-consuming  
- Expensive  
- Impractical or impossible  

Therefore, we study a sample and apply inferential statistics to reach conclusions.

---

### Example
A company wants to know whether people like a new mobile phone.

- Population: All customers (1,00,000)
- Sample: 300 customers

If most of the 300 customers like the phone, the company assumes that **most customers in the population will like it**.

This decision is made using inferential statistics.

---

### Exam-Oriented Definition
Inferential statistics is used to make predictions, decisions, or conclusions about a population based on sample data.

---

## 2. Population and Sample

---

### Population
Population refers to the **entire group of individuals or items** that we want to study.

#### Examples:
- All students in a university  
- All voters in a country  
- All products manufactured in a factory  

---

### Sample
A sample is a **small selected part of the population** used for analysis.

#### Examples:
- 200 students selected from a university  
- 1,000 voters surveyed from a city  
- 50 products tested from a factory  

---

### Why Do We Use a Sample?
- Studying the entire population takes more time  
- It costs more money  
- Managing large data is difficult  

Using a sample makes the study easier and faster.

---

### Example
- Population: 10,000 students  
- Sample: 250 students  

Information from 250 students is used to understand all 10,000 students.

---

## 3. Sampling

---

### Meaning
Sampling is the **process of selecting a sample from the population**.

Good sampling gives accurate results.  
Poor sampling leads to wrong conclusions.

---

### Types of Sampling (Basic)

---

#### Random Sampling
Every member of the population has an **equal chance of being selected**.

Example:  
Selecting student roll numbers using a lottery method.

---

#### Stratified Sampling
The population is divided into **groups**, and samples are taken from each group.

Example:  
A college has Arts, Science, and Commerce students.  
Students are selected from each stream to form a sample.

---

#### Systematic Sampling
Selection is done at **fixed intervals**.

Example:  
Every 10th customer entering a mall is selected for a survey.

---

## 4. Sampling Error

---

### Meaning
Sampling error is the **difference between the result obtained from a sample and the actual result of the population**.

---

### Example
- Sample average marks: 68  
- Actual population average marks: 70  

Difference = Sampling error

A small sampling error is normal.

---

## 5. Sampling Bias

---

### Meaning
Sampling bias occurs when the sample is **not selected fairly**, and some members of the population are ignored.

---

### Example
- Asking only toppers whether an exam was easy  
- Surveying only city people about village problems  

Such samples give incorrect or misleading results.

---

## Summary of Part 1

- Inferential statistics helps in decision-making  
- Population is the entire group  
- Sample is a small part of the population  
- Sampling should be fair  
- Bias and large sampling error lead to wrong conclusions
