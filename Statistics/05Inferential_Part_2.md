## 1. Probability

### Meaning 
Probability tells us **how likely an event is to happen**.

It helps us make predictions when the outcome is uncertain.

---

### Probability Values
- Probability always lies between **0 and 1**
- 0 means the event will not happen
- 1 means the event will definitely happen

---

### Example
- Probability of getting heads when tossing a coin = 1/2  
- Probability of rain tomorrow = 0.6 (60%)

---

### Role of Probability in Inferential Statistics
Probability helps us:
- Measure uncertainty  
- Make decisions based on chance  
- Perform hypothesis testing  

---

## 2. Probability Distribution

---

### Meaning
A probability distribution shows **how values of a variable are spread** and **how often they occur**.

---

### Simple Example
Consider marks obtained by students:

| Marks | Number of Students |
|------|-------------------|
| 40   | 2 |
| 50   | 5 |
| 60   | 8 |
| 70   | 5 |

This table shows the **distribution of marks**.

---

### Common Types of Distributions (Introduction)
- Normal distribution  
- Binomial distribution  

Distributions help us understand patterns in data.

---

## 3. Hypothesis and Testing

---

### Hypothesis

A hypothesis is a **statement or assumption** that we want to test using data.

---

### Types of Hypothesis

#### Null Hypothesis (H₀)
- Assumes **no effect or no difference**
- It is the default assumption

Example:  
There is no difference between online and offline exam results.

---

#### Alternative Hypothesis (H₁)
- Opposite of the null hypothesis  
- Assumes there **is an effect or difference**

Example:  
There is a difference between online and offline exam results.

---

### Hypothesis Testing (Basic Steps)
1. State the hypothesis  
2. Collect sample data  
3. Analyze the data  
4. Accept or reject the null hypothesis  

---

### Simple Example
A company claims that the average weight of a packet is 1 kg.

- H₀: Average weight = 1 kg  
- H₁: Average weight ≠ 1 kg  

Sample packets are tested to check the claim.

---

## 4. Correlation

---

### Meaning
Correlation shows the **relationship between two variables**.

It tells us whether variables move together.

---

### Types of Correlation
- Positive correlation  
- Negative correlation  
- Zero correlation  

---

### Examples
- Height and weight: positive correlation  
- Speed and travel time: negative correlation  
- Shoe size and marks: zero correlation  

---

## 5. Covariance

---

### Meaning
Covariance also measures the **relationship between two variables**, but it shows **direction**, not strength.

---

### Interpretation
- Positive covariance: variables move in the same direction  
- Negative covariance: variables move in opposite directions  

---

### Example
If study hours increase and marks increase, covariance is positive.

---

# Difference Between Correlation and Covariance

| Basis | Correlation | Covariance |
|------|-------------|------------|
| Meaning | Shows the relationship between two variables | Shows how two variables change together |
| What it tells | Direction and strength of the relationship | Only direction of the relationship |
| Value range | Lies between -1 and +1 | No fixed range |
| Unit | Unit-free (no unit) | Has units of the variables |
| Interpretation | Easy to understand and compare | Difficult to interpret |
| Standardization | Standardized measure | Not standardized |
| Usage | Used to measure how strong the relationship is | Used to know the direction of movement |
| Example result | r = 0.8 (strong positive relation) | Covariance = 150 (direction known, strength unclear) |

---

## Simple Example to Understand

If **study hours** and **marks** increase together:
- Covariance → Positive  
- Correlation → Positive, and strength can be measured  

---

## In Short

- Correlation tells how strongly and in which direction variables are related  
- Covariance tells only the direction of relationship
