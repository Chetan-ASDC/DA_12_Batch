# Descriptive Statistics

## 1. What is Descriptive Statistics?

**Descriptive Statistics** is a branch of statistics that deals with  
**collecting, organizing, summarizing, and presenting data** in a meaningful way.

It helps us understand **what the data shows** by using numbers, tables, or charts,  
without making predictions about future outcomes.

 It answers: **“What is happening in the data?”**

---

## 2. Applications of Descriptive Statistics  


### 1. Education
Used to summarize student performance.

**Example:**  
A teacher calculates the **average marks**, **highest marks**, and **lowest marks** of a class to understand overall performance.

---

### 2. Business
Used to analyze sales and profit data.

**Example:**  
A shop owner calculates **average daily sales** and identifies the **most sold product** in a month.

---

### 3. Healthcare
Used to summarize patient data.

**Example:**  
A hospital finds the **average number of days patients stay** in the hospital.

---

### 4. Data Analysis & Research
Used to understand large datasets before deeper analysis.

**Example:**  
A data analyst calculates **average website visitors per day** and identifies peak traffic days.

---

### 5. Government
Used to summarize population or economic data.

**Example:**  
Government reports show **average income**, **population count**, and **literacy rate**.

---

## 3. Terms of Descriptive Statistics  

### A. Measures of Central Tendency

These measures show the **central or typical value** of data.

---

### 1. Mean (Average)
The arithmetic average of all values.

**Real-World Example:**  
Average salary of employees in a company.

---

### 2. Median (Middle Value)
The middle value when data is arranged in order.

**Real-World Example:**  
Median household income used by governments to avoid the effect of very high incomes.

---

### 3. Mode (Most Frequent Value)
The value that appears most often.

**Real-World Example:**  
Most common shoe size sold in a store.

---

### B. Measures of Dispersion

These measures show **how spread out the data is**.

---

### 4. Range
Difference between the highest and lowest values.

**Real-World Example:**  
Difference between highest and lowest temperature in a day.

---

### 5. Standard Deviation
Shows how much data values differ from the average.

**Real-World Example:**  
Consistency of marks in a class—low standard deviation means students scored similar marks.

---

## 4. Importance of Descriptive Statistics in Data Analysis

- Converts raw data into meaningful information  
- Helps identify patterns and trends  
- Makes data easy to understand for decision-makers  
- Forms the base for inferential statistics  

---

## 5. Summary

- Descriptive statistics summarizes data  
- Used in education, business, healthcare, and research  
- Mean, median, mode show central tendency  
- Range and standard deviation show variability  
- First and most important step in data analysis
